Stanford Open Datathon, April 2021

Identifying COVID-19 Misinformation in order to Combat it

Twitter data highlights key pandemic misconceptions that could be key in ensuring an informed public

Masaya Heywood, Kopal Mathur, Teddy Sandler, Jenna Simon, and Jacob Valdez

An "Infodemic"

The World Health Organization coined the term "Infodemic" to represent the spread of inaccurate or incorrect information during a pandemic outbreak [1]. With the spread of misinformation over the past year, there is growing concern that people will distrust the advice of legitimate sources and engage in activity that could be detrimental to general health and public well-being.

With the overwhelming spread of inaccurate information regarding the pandemic on public platforms (such as social media), there is an increasing need to be able to identify prevalent false statements so that they can be countered with facts. By analyzing Twitter data since March 2020, we are able to identify the rise and fall of negative sentiments towards the COVID-19 vaccine, and the most prevalent anti-vaccination beliefs that are likely to contribute to these stances. By isolating these beliefs, public officials can focus efforts on countering the specific pieces of misinformation that are likely to have the most harmful effect on public health.

Negative Sentiment towards the COVID-19 Vaccine Persists

As reported by the New York Times in December 2020, more than 25% of Americans "probably or definitely would not take a coronavirus vaccine" [2]. A June 2020 survey by Nature Medicine shows significant fluctuations in citizens' willingness to take the vaccine when broken down by country. 88.62% of Chinese citizens said they would take the vaccine, while only 54.85% of Russian citizens would be willing to do so [3].

Data collected from the social media platform Twitter reflects this continued hesitancy to trust a COVID-19 vaccination. Sentiment analysis of tweets with the "COVIDVaccine" hashtag since March 2020 show continuous negative feelings towards the immunization.

Additional Resources for Fact Checking and Fighting Misinformation

  • PolitiFact
    Checks the accuracy of claims made by politicians.

  • Poynter Fact-Check
    PolitiFact's parent company. Fact checker made to educate journalists and the public.

  • Snopes.com
    Investigative reporting for fact-checking and evidence-based analysis. Self described "internet's definitive fact-checking resource."

  • The Washington Post Fact Checker
    Focuses specifically on checking political facts and claims in-depth.

  • Web Literacy for Student Fact Checkers
    Free e-book by Michael A. Caulfield. A complete fact-checking guide written for students.
